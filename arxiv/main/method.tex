\section{Methodology} % (fold)
\label{sec:method}

%The primary thing to consider to convert the shots charts into functional datasets. 
The first step in our approach is to convert the raw shots charts into smooth functions for analysis. 
To achieve this, we employ a 2-dimensional kernel density estimation for both the attempted and made shots, utilizing Silverman's rule \cite{silvermanDensityEstimationStatistics1986} for the estimation of the bandwidth. 
The density estimation is conducted on a equally-spaced grid of $201 \times 201$ points.
We then treat the smoothed attempted and made shots densities for each player as the two features of a bivariate functional observation, where both features are defined on a two-dimensional rectangular domain, and observed on an equidistant grid of size $201 \times 201$. Figure~\ref{fig:examples_shooting_density} presents the density estimates of the same five players displayed in the shots charts in Figure~\ref{fig:examples_shooting_charts}. 

We then employ multivariate functional principal components analysis (MFPCA), developed in \cite{golovkineUseGramMatrix2024}, to decompose each estimated density into a common set of basis of functions and player-specific basis coefficients (i.e., scores). 
Let $X$ denote an observation of the density. 
We aim to find the basis functions $\{\phi_k\}_{1 \leq k \leq K}$ that provide the most parsimonious representation of $X$ as 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kl_decomp}
    X(s, t) = \mu(s, t) + \sum_{k = 1}^K c_k\phi_k(s, t), \quad s, t \in ...,
\end{equation}
where $\mu(s, t)$ is the mean (average) function, and $c_k$ are the player-specific basis coefficients, known as the scores.
The weighted sum in Equation \eqref{eq:kl_decomp} means that we can view each eigenfunction as characterising a shift away from the mean function for each player, where the player-specific score defines the magnitude and direction of the shift.
We choose to estimate four components ($K = 4$) as these explain around $90\%$ of the variance in the data. 
The scores $c_k$ are then estimated as the projection of the observations onto the eigenfunctions.
These scores provide a finite-dimensional representation of the functional data and can be used as scalar variables in subsequent analysis, as they capture player-specific characteristics.
To create a typology of players, we run a $k$-means clustering algorithm (\cite{macqueenMethodsClassificationAnalysis1967}) with $5$ clusters, as the number of players on the court.
