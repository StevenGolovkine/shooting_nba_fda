\section{Methodology} % (fold)
\label{sec:method}

The first step in our approach is to convert the raw shots charts into smooth functions suitable for analysis. To achieve this, we use a two-dimensional kernel density estimator with a Gaussian kernel for both missed and made shots. After normalizing the court to the $[0, 1] \times [0, 1]$ range, bandwidth estimation is performed utilizing Silverman's rule \citep{silvermanDensityEstimationStatistics1986}. The density estimation is conducted on a regularly sampled grid of $201 \times 201$ points. We then represent the smoothed densities of missed and made shots for each player as the two features of a bivariate functional observation. Both features are defined on the same two-dimensional rectangular domain and observed over the $201 \times 201$ grid. Note that the choice of the grid resolution is arbitrary and can be adjusted as needed. Figure~\ref{fig:examples_shooting_density} shows the density estimates of the same five players whose shots charts are presented in Figure~\ref{fig:examples_shooting_charts}. 

We apply multivariate functional principal components analysis (MFPCA) \citep{happMultivariateFunctionalPrincipal2018} to decompose each estimated density into a common set of basis functions and player-specific coefficients. Let $X$ denote an observation of the densities of a player, represented as a vector of two functions: the first corresponding to the density of missed shots and the second to the density of the made shots. We aim to find basis functions $\{\phi_k\}_{1 \leq k \leq K}$ that provide the most parsimonious representation of $X$, for a given $K$, as 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kl_decomp}
    X(s, t) = \mu(s, t) + \sum_{k = 1}^K c_k\phi_k(s, t), \quad s, t \in [0, 1] \times [0, 1],
\end{equation}
where $\mu(s, t)$ is the mean (average) function, and $c_k$ are the player-specific basis coefficients, referred to as scores. Each eigenfunction in the decomposition~\eqref{eq:kl_decomp} characterise a variation away from the mean function, with the player-specific scores indicating the magnitude and direction of the deviation for each individual player. We estimate four components ($K = 4$), based on the approach of \citep{golovkineUseGramMatrix2024}, explaining approximately $90\%$ of the total variance in the data. The scores $c_k$ are estimated as the projection of the observations $X$ onto the eigenfunctions $\phi_k$. These scores provide a finite-dimensional representation of the functional data, capturing player-specific characteristics, and can be used as scalar variables in subsequent analyses. 
To create a data-driven taxonomy of players based on MFPCA, we apply a $k$-medoids clustering algorithm \citep{kaufmanFindingGroupsData2009} to the normalised functional principal component scores $c_k$, using $k = 5$ clusters, as the number of players on the court. The $k$-medoids algorithm minimize the dissimilarity between each player's scores and the medoid of its cluster. We use the weighted euclidean distance as the measure of dissimilarity. This method offers a practical advantage over $k$-means clustering: the center of each cluster is an actual player from the dataset rather than an averaged representation of all players within the cluster, enhancing interpretability. In our analysis, we consider two weighting schemes to calculate the distance: one assigns equal weights to all components, and the other weights components given the proportion of variance they explain.
